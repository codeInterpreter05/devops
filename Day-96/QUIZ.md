# Day 96 — Quiz: SLOs, SLAs & Error Budgets

Try to answer without looking at your notes. Answers are at the bottom.

1. Define SLI and SLO in one sentence each, and explain how they relate to each other.
2. Why should an SLI ideally be measured as close to the user's actual experience as possible, rather than purely from server-side status codes?
3. What practical problem occurs if your SLO's latency threshold doesn't line up with any of your histogram's bucket boundaries?
4. Why are rolling windows generally preferred over calendar-aligned windows for SLOs?
5. Calculate: for a 99.95% SLO over a 30-day rolling window, how many minutes of "badness" are allowed? Show the formula.
6. Why does going from 99.9% to 99.99% represent roughly a 10x harder engineering problem, not just "a bit harder"?
7. What is burn rate, and why is it a better thing to alert on than "error budget already exhausted"?
8. Explain why a single-window burn-rate alert is a bad design, and how multi-window alerting fixes it.
9. What's the difference between an SLO and an SLA, and why is the SLO usually set tighter than the SLA?
10. Give two concrete reasons SLO-based (burn-rate) alerting produces better on-call signal than plain threshold alerting.
11. What does Sloth do, and what does Nobl9 add on top of what a tool like Sloth provides?
12. **Interview question:** What is an error budget and how do you use it to make deployment decisions?

---

## Answers

1. SLI: a quantitative measurement of some aspect of service quality (e.g., the ratio of successful to total requests), always expressed over a time window. SLO: a target value or range for that SLI (e.g., "99.9% over 30 days"). The SLO is meaningless without a precisely defined SLI to measure against, and the SLI alone has no notion of "good enough" without the SLO's target.
2. Because a server can return a "successful" status code (HTTP 200) while the actual user experience was bad — e.g., the response took so long the user gave up, or the response body contained a client-visible error the server doesn't consider a failure. Measuring close to the user (client-side/edge/RUM) captures what users actually experienced, not just what the server internally logged as fine.
3. The SLI query can't be computed precisely — e.g., if your SLO threshold is "under 300ms" but your histogram buckets are `[0.1, 0.5, 1, 5]`, there's no bucket boundary at 0.3s, so you either have to approximate (using a nearby bucket) or re-bucket the histogram. This is why SLO thresholds should be decided before finalizing histogram bucket boundaries.
4. Calendar-aligned windows (e.g., strict calendar months) create an artifact where reliability effectively "resets" on the 1st of the month, making a bad incident near the end of a period look far less severe (spread over few remaining days) than the same incident near the start. Rolling windows continuously slide, giving an honest, continuously-current picture without that reset artifact.
5. `30 days × 24h × 60min × (1 - 0.9995) = 43,200 × 0.0005 = 21.6` minutes (commonly cited as ~21.9 minutes using a 30.44-day average month). Formula: `window_minutes × (1 − SLO_target)`.
6. Because the allowed downtime (error budget) shrinks by roughly 10x for each additional "nine" (99.9% → 43.8 min/month allowed; 99.99% → 4.38 min/month allowed) — sustaining a 10x-smaller margin for error typically requires disproportionately more engineering investment (deeper redundancy, faster automated failover, more rigorous testing/chaos engineering, tighter on-call response), not a linear increase in effort.
7. Burn rate is the speed at which the error budget is being consumed relative to a sustainable pace (burn rate 1 = exactly sustainable, lands at the SLO target by window's end). It's better to alert on than "budget exhausted" because burn rate is a leading indicator — it warns you *before* the budget actually runs out, giving time to react, whereas alerting on exhaustion is a lagging indicator that fires only after the SLO has already been violated.
8. A short window alone detects fast severe outages quickly but is noisy (fires on brief harmless blips); a long window alone is stable but far too slow to catch a sudden severe outage (may take many hours to notice). Multi-window alerting requires *both* a short window and a long window to simultaneously exceed the burn-rate threshold before firing — the short window gives fast detection, the long window confirms the degradation is sustained rather than a transient blip, cutting false positives while preserving fast detection of real incidents.
9. SLA is an external, contractual commitment to customers with financial/legal consequences for violation (e.g., service credits); SLO is an internal, operational target with no direct financial penalty. The SLO is set tighter than the SLA to create a safety margin — you want internal alerts to fire and give the team time to react before performance degrades enough to actually breach the (looser) contractual SLA.
10. (1) Burn-rate alerts are relative to each service's own agreed error budget, so the same raw error rate is correctly treated as a non-event for a loose-SLO service and a serious event for a tight-SLO service, unlike a one-size-fits-all threshold. (2) Burn-rate alerts encode urgency directly via severity tiers (e.g., a 14.4x burn rate pages immediately because budget would be gone in ~2 days; a 3x burn rate only opens a ticket because there's about a week of runway) — a flat threshold alert gives no sense of how urgently to respond.
11. Sloth is an open-source tool that takes a declarative SLO spec (SLI queries, target, alerting config) and auto-generates the corresponding Prometheus recording rules and multi-window burn-rate alert rules, codifying the error-budget math so teams don't hand-write it per service. Nobl9 is a commercial, vendor-agnostic SaaS platform that does the equivalent across many different backend telemetry sources (not just Prometheus) — Datadog, CloudWatch, BigQuery, etc. — with unified dashboards and reporting across an organization, useful when telemetry isn't centralized on one Prometheus-compatible stack.
12. Strong answer: "An error budget is the inverse of the SLO — if the SLO is 99.9% availability, the error budget is the remaining 0.1% of allowed 'badness,' expressible as a wall-clock time (43.8 minutes/month at 99.9%). It converts reliability from an open-ended goal into a finite, spendable resource. In practice, my team would use it as a pre-agreed policy: if the budget isn't exhausted, we have room to ship faster and take on some risk (more frequent deploys, experiments); if it's exhausted or a burn-rate alert shows we're on pace to exhaust it soon, we freeze non-critical releases and redirect engineering effort toward reliability work until the budget recovers. The key is that this policy is agreed upon by product and engineering leadership ahead of time, so the decision to slow down releases is driven by data, not a heated argument during an incident."
