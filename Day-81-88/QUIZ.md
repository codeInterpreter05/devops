# Day 81-88 — Quiz: Phase 2 Consolidation & Interview Prep

Try to answer out loud, as if to an interviewer, before looking at the answers. These are deliberately system-design and judgment questions, not one-line recall — grade yourself on whether you gave a *reasoned* answer, not just a correct-sounding term.

1. A teammate proposes rebuilding the application from source separately for staging and production, arguing "it lets us catch environment-specific build issues early." What's wrong with this approach from a supply-chain and consistency standpoint, and what would you propose instead?
2. You're asked to add a canary deployment to a service that currently deploys via a simple rolling update. What three things does canary *require* that a basic rolling update doesn't, and what happens if you skip the automated metrics-based promotion/abort step?
3. Walk through what happens, end to end, when a security scanner finds a critical CVE in a dependency already running in production — where in your pipeline/platform would this get caught if it had been caught *before* release, and what's your response process now that it's already deployed?
4. Your platform team wants to add a new mandatory OPA policy that blocks any container running as root. How would you roll this out to 500 engineers' pipelines without causing a wave of broken builds the next morning?
5. Explain the difference between push-based deployment and GitOps (pull-based) deployment, and give one concrete security reason GitOps is generally preferred for production clusters.
6. A team's staging environment has drifted from what's declared in the GitOps config repo — someone manually patched a resource with `kubectl edit` under time pressure during an incident. What are the operational and cultural (not just technical) implications of this, and how would you prevent it happening again?
7. **Behavioral:** Tell me about a time you had to explain a technical decision (a pipeline design, a security gate, a deployment strategy) to someone non-technical or from another team who pushed back on it. What did you do, and what was the outcome? (Structure this as STAR — Situation, Task, Action, Result.)
8. Your CI pipeline for a 500-engineer org is taking 25 minutes per build and teams are complaining. Walk through at least three distinct levers you'd pull to reduce this, and explain what each one trades off.
9. Compare SAST, SCA, and DAST: what does each catch that the others don't, and why does the *order* they run in the pipeline matter?
10. **Behavioral:** Describe a mistake you made in a CI/CD, GitOps, or deployment context (a lab exercise counts) — what went wrong, what you did once you noticed, and what you changed afterward so it wouldn't happen again.
11. If you had to justify to a skeptical engineering leader why the platform team needs 5-10 dedicated engineers instead of "just let each team manage its own CI/CD," what's your strongest argument, and what's the strongest counterargument you'd concede has some truth to it?
12. **Interview question (verbatim):** Design a complete DevOps platform for a 200-person engineering organisation from scratch.

## Answers

1. Rebuilding per environment reintroduces exactly the risk it's trying to prevent: a different build (different dependency resolution timing, different base image patch level, different compiler/toolchain state) can behave differently even from "the same" source, which is the classic "works in staging, breaks in prod" bug — except now it's *worse* because the artifacts were never actually identical, breaking any provenance/SBOM claim about what's running in prod. The fix: build the artifact once, immutably (tag/digest it), scan and sign it once, and promote that exact same artifact through dev → staging → prod. Environment-specific behavior should come from *configuration* injected at deploy time, not from rebuilding the code.
2. Canary requires: (1) **traffic splitting** — the ability to route a small percentage of real traffic to the new version while the rest hits the old version; (2) **version-scoped metrics** — error rate/latency/saturation measured specifically for the canary's traffic slice, not aggregate service health; (3) **automated promotion/abort logic** tied to those metrics (or at minimum, a human decision gate with clear go/no-go criteria) — without this, you're just running a slow rolling update with extra steps. Skipping automated promotion/abort means a bad canary can sit serving a small slice of broken traffic indefinitely if no one is watching closely, or someone has to manually notice and intervene under time pressure — which defeats the purpose of canary as a *safety* mechanism.
3. If caught pre-release: SCA (software composition analysis) scanning the dependency manifest during CI would flag it before the artifact is even built/signed, blocking the pipeline. Post-deployment response: use the SBOM to identify every running artifact that includes the vulnerable dependency version (this is exactly what an SBOM is for — answering "where is this used" fast, without hunting through every repo manually), assess actual exploitability/exposure, patch and re-release through the normal pipeline (not a manual hotfix that skips gates), and add/verify a policy so newly-scanned CVEs of this severity block future builds automatically.
4. Roll it out in **warn/audit mode first** — the policy logs violations without blocking, giving teams visibility into what would break. Communicate the upcoming enforcement date and provide the specific list of currently-non-compliant workloads to each affected team. Give a deprecation window (days to weeks depending on org size) before flipping the policy to blocking/enforcing mode. This avoids the classic mistake of silently switching to strict enforcement and breaking every non-compliant team's deploys with no warning.
5. Push-based deployment means CI (or a human) actively pushes changes into the cluster — which requires cluster credentials to live outside the cluster (in CI, on a laptop), widening the credential attack surface. GitOps (pull-based) means an in-cluster operator (Argo CD/Flux) continuously pulls the desired state from a Git repo and reconciles the live cluster to match — cluster credentials never need to leave the cluster, and the operator can independently detect and correct drift even if something changes the cluster out-of-band. The concrete security reason: **no external system needs write credentials to the cluster**, shrinking the blast radius if CI itself is ever compromised.
6. Technically, drift means the cluster no longer matches the declared source of truth, so the next reconciliation could either silently revert the incident fix (if the operator auto-corrects) or the drift could persist invisibly (if reconciliation is paused/ignored), either of which is dangerous. Culturally, it reflects a real gap: under incident pressure, the "proper" GitOps path (commit, PR, merge, wait for reconciliation) was slower than the team needed, so they bypassed it — that's a signal the emergency-change process itself needs a sanctioned fast path (e.g., a pre-approved emergency-merge process with retroactive review), not just a reminder to "follow the process next time." Prevention: make the sanctioned fast path actually fast enough to use under pressure, and treat any manual cluster edit as something that must be reconciled back into Git immediately after, not left as silent drift.
7. There's no single correct answer — grade your own response against the rubric in CHEATSHEET.md: did you use a clear Situation/Task/Action/Result structure, did you focus on your own actions (not just "the team decided"), and did you state a concrete, ideally quantified, result? A strong answer names the specific pushback, the specific reasoning you used to address it (not "I just explained it better"), and a specific outcome (the decision stuck, the process changed, or you learned something and changed your approach next time).
8. Reasonable levers, each with a real tradeoff: (a) **parallelize test suites** (matrix/sharding) — trades more concurrent runner capacity/cost for wall-clock speed; (b) **cache dependencies/build layers** keyed by lockfile hash — trades cache storage and occasional cache-invalidation bugs for skipping redundant work; (c) **split the pipeline so only affected paths rebuild** (path-based triggers in a monorepo, or smaller service boundaries) — trades some pipeline complexity for not rebuilding everything on every change; (d) **move slow/non-blocking checks (e.g., some scans) to run async/post-merge** rather than blocking every PR — trades some delayed signal for faster feedback on the critical path. A strong answer picks at least three and explicitly states what each costs, not just what it saves.
9. SAST scans source code statically (pre-build) and catches code-level issues like injection flaws or unsafe deserialization, but can't see issues that only manifest at runtime and doesn't know about third-party library vulnerabilities. SCA scans your dependency manifest/lockfile for known-CVE libraries — it catches vulnerable dependencies SAST wouldn't touch (SAST looks at *your* code, not the libraries you pulled in) but won't catch a vulnerability you introduced yourself. DAST tests a *running* application from the outside (black-box), catching issues that only appear in a live environment (misconfigurations, runtime auth issues) but running much later and more slowly than SAST/SCA. Order matters because SAST and SCA are fast and cheap and can run on every commit pre-build, failing fast before expensive steps; DAST needs a running instance and is slower, so it typically runs later (e.g., against a staging deploy) — running DAST first would waste time deploying code that a 30-second SAST scan would have already rejected.
10. No single correct answer — but a strong response names a *specific* real mistake (even from a lab, e.g., "I ran `rm -rf` with an unquoted variable in a cleanup script and it deleted more than intended" or "I merged a GitOps config change straight to the prod branch without realizing it skipped the approval gate"), states what you did immediately after noticing (not what you *should* have done — what you *actually* did), and states a concrete change you made afterward (a script safeguard, a branch protection rule, a personal checklist) so it's clear you learned something structural, not just "I'll be more careful."
11. Strongest argument for a dedicated platform team: without centralized ownership of shared runners, golden-path templates, and mandatory security gates, you get 500 engineers independently reinventing (and mis-configuring) the same pipeline logic, inconsistent security posture across teams, and no single place to fix a systemic issue (e.g., a new supply-chain requirement) without touching hundreds of individual pipeline files. The strongest fair counterargument: a centralized team *can* become a bottleneck and a single point of frustration if it's under-resourced relative to the org, or if it over-centralizes decisions that individual teams are better positioned to make about their own service's specific needs — which is exactly why the golden-path model (centrally own the shared foundation, let teams own their service-specific logic) is the standard middle ground, not full centralization.
12. **Worked answer** — treat this as the same platform question as "500 engineers" but scaled to a 200-person org building the platform for the *first* time (greenfield), which changes the emphasis:
    - **Clarify first**: existing tooling or fully greenfield? Cloud provider? Regulatory constraints? Current pain (or is this a brand-new org)? At 200 people, assume a handful of platform-focused engineers can be dedicated, but the org is small enough that full self-service tooling (a Backstage-style catalog) may be over-engineering relative to a simpler shared template repo — call this out as a scale-appropriate judgment call.
    - **Source control + CI**: pick a managed CI (GitHub Actions/GitLab CI) rather than building custom orchestration — at this size, differentiate on golden-path templates and policy, not on the CI engine itself.
    - **Artifact registry + immutable builds**: build once, tag by commit SHA/digest, promote the same artifact through environments.
    - **Security gates from day one, not bolted on later**: secret scanning and SCA are nearly free to add at this stage and are far cheaper to establish as a norm now than to retrofit onto 200 engineers' habits later.
    - **GitOps for deployment**: even at 200 people, the audit trail and rollback-via-revert benefits are worth the upfront setup cost versus ad hoc deploy scripts, especially since "we'll add it later" tends to never actually happen once teams are used to manual deploys.
    - **Observability from day one**: centralized logging/metrics/tracing baseline (even a modest managed stack) so every team isn't independently solving "how do I know if my service is healthy."
    - **Ownership model**: a small (2-5 person) platform team owns the shared templates, CI/CD infrastructure, and security gates; product teams own their service code and deployment cadence within those guardrails — the same platform-team-enables/product-teams-own split as the 500-engineer answer, just sized down.
    - **Tradeoffs to state explicitly**: at 200 people, over-investing in enterprise-scale tooling (a full internal developer platform, highly bespoke policy engines) before it's needed is itself a risk — cost and maintenance burden on a small platform team can outpace the org's actual need; the right answer sizes the platform to *today's* scale while choosing components (GitOps, immutable artifacts, security gates) that scale forward without a rebuild later.
