# Day 97 — Quiz: Incident Management

Try to answer without looking at your notes. Answers are at the bottom.

1. What distinguishes a P1 from a P2 incident? Give a concrete example of each.
2. Why should severity be treated as revisable during an incident rather than fixed at declaration?
3. What's the difference between acknowledging and resolving a page, and why does conflating them cause problems?
4. Why is the Incident Commander role kept separate from the hands-on-keyboard responder role?
5. Name three properties of a runbook that make it likely to actually be followed under pressure (vs. abandoned).
6. What's the loose distinction between a runbook and a playbook?
7. Why does "blameless" matter for the *accuracy* of a postmortem, not just for morale?
8. Walk through a 5 Whys example for an incident of your choosing, ending on a systemic (not personal) cause.
9. What's a key limitation of 5 Whys, and what technique can supplement it?
10. What five sections should a solid postmortem document contain at minimum?
11. Why is "add more monitoring" a weak postmortem action item, and what would a stronger version look like?
12. **Interview question:** Walk me through how you'd handle a P1 production incident from detection to postmortem.

---

## Answers

1. P1: complete outage or critical business function down, affecting most/all users, no workaround (e.g., payment processing down site-wide). P2: significant degradation or a major feature broken affecting a meaningful subset of users, workaround may exist (e.g., one region down, or checkout badly degraded but not fully down). The distinguishing factors are blast radius, business criticality, and workaround availability.
2. Because the true scope of an incident is often unclear at the moment it's first noticed — an issue that looks like minor "elevated errors on one endpoint" (P3) can turn out to be the payment gateway itself (P1) once investigated further. Locking severity at the initial guess risks under-resourcing a genuinely critical incident or over-escalating a minor one.
3. Acknowledging tells the escalation system "a human is aware and working on it," which stops further escalation; resolving marks the underlying issue as actually fixed. Conflating them (e.g., not acknowledging through the tool because you're "already aware" informally) lets the escalation timer keep running and can unnecessarily page additional tiers of people.
4. Because coordination/communication (tracking status, deciding severity, managing stakeholder updates, deciding when to escalate) and deep hands-on technical debugging are both full-focus jobs — the same person doing both during a serious incident means one of them (usually communication) gets neglected, causing stakeholders to interrupt the debugger directly for status, fragmenting their focus at the worst time.
5. Any three of: exact copy-pasteable commands (not prose descriptions); a clear, unambiguous trigger condition at the top; diagnosis steps with expected output before remediation steps; an explicit escalation/rollback path if the runbook doesn't resolve the issue; being linked directly from the alert itself; being kept up to date (treated as a first-class artifact, updated whenever an incident reveals a gap).
6. A runbook is a specific, mechanical, nearly-verbatim procedure for a known failure mode or routine task (e.g., "restart the stuck Kafka consumer group"). A playbook is a broader strategic/judgment framework for a category of situation (e.g., "how to run any P1 incident," "how to handle a security breach") — less exact commands, more decision framework. Teams often use the terms loosely/interchangeably in practice.
7. If engineers believe an honest account could get them blamed or punished, they will rationally hedge, omit details, or avoid volunteering the full timeline — exactly the information needed to find the true systemic cause. Blame culture therefore makes postmortems *less accurate*, not just less pleasant; blameless framing is what gets people to disclose the full, honest picture.
8. Example structure (any valid domain-appropriate chain is acceptable): Why #1 (proximate technical symptom) → Why #2 (deeper technical cause) → Why #3 (why did it reach production — e.g., a testing gap) → Why #4 (why does that testing gap exist — e.g., no standard requiring that kind of test) → Why #5 (systemic, process-level cause — e.g., team testing standards don't cover this failure category), with the final action item targeting the systemic gap, not an individual's mistake.
9. 5 Whys assumes a single linear causal chain, but real incidents are often multi-causal (several independent contributing factors have to align, not just one chain of causes) — rigidly forcing one "why" per level can miss parallel contributing factors. A contributing-factors analysis (sometimes visualized as a fishbone/Ishikawa diagram) can supplement it by capturing multiple independent factors rather than one linear path.
10. Summary (what happened, impact, duration, severity), Timeline (precise, timestamped, sourced from logs/chat not memory), Root cause(s) (via structured analysis like 5 Whys), Impact (quantified — users/requests affected, error-budget consumption), and Action items (owned, dated, tracked to completion) — many postmortems also include an honest "what went well / what went poorly" section.
11. "Add more monitoring" is weak because it's not specific or trackable — nobody can verify later whether it was actually done or whether it would have caught the issue. A stronger version names the exact signal, threshold, and owner: e.g., "add a burn-rate alert on `checkout-api`'s DB connection pool utilization exceeding 80% for 10 minutes, owned by the platform team, due by end of sprint" — concrete enough to verify completion and effectiveness.
12. Strong answer: On detection (page fires or symptom noticed), quickly assess and declare severity (P1: complete outage/critical function down, no workaround), acknowledge the page through the tool to stop further escalation, and immediately establish roles — an Incident Commander for coordination/comms, separate from the hands-on responder(s) actually debugging. Open a dedicated incident channel for low-noise technical coordination, and a separate stakeholder-facing channel/status page for regular plain-language updates (even "still investigating" updates, to avoid stakeholders interrupting responders directly). Follow the relevant runbook if one exists, checking diagnosis steps against expected output before jumping to remediation; escalate further (more people, wider incident) if the runbook doesn't resolve it within a reasonable window. Once user-facing impact is confirmed resolved (sustained baseline, not just a brief improvement), declare resolution and communicate it. Afterward, hold a blameless postmortem: build a precise timeline from logs/chat, run a 5-Whys (or contributing-factors) analysis to find the systemic root cause(s) rather than blaming an individual, quantify impact including error-budget consumption, and produce concrete, owned, dated action items that get tracked to completion in normal engineering work — including updating or creating the runbook if this incident revealed a gap.
