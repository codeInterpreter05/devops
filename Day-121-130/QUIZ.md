# Day 121-130 — Quiz: Multi-Cloud & Edge

Try to answer without notes — several of these ask for a real mechanism or a real number, not just a term. Answers are at the bottom.

1. What is the concrete difference between a zonal and a regional GKE cluster, and why does it matter operationally beyond "one is more available"?
2. EKS charges a per-cluster hourly control-plane fee that GKE Standard mode doesn't have in the same form. Name two other concrete ways EKS's control-plane abstraction is "thinner" than GKE's.
3. Explain the actual difference in scope between GKE Autopilot and EKS Fargate profiles — they're often described as equivalent, but they aren't the same shape of tradeoff. What is that difference?
4. A team sizes their EKS node group purely on CPU/memory and later can't schedule any more pods on nodes that still have spare CPU/memory. What's the most likely cause, and what's the fix?
5. What problem do GKE Workload Identity and EKS IRSA both solve, and what is the one concrete difference in how the trust relationship is established between the two?
6. What is a "fleet" (Anthos) or an "Arc-enabled resource" (Azure), and why is neither concept just "a cluster with a different name"?
7. Explain what Cluster API's management cluster actually does, and why `clusterctl move` (pivoting) exists.
8. In multi-cluster Istio, what specifically causes cross-cluster mTLS to fail even when remote secrets are exchanged correctly and both east-west gateways are healthy? What's the fix?
9. A `DestinationRule` has `localityLbSetting.failover` configured but no `outlierDetection`. What happens during a real local outage, and why?
10. Why is Cloudflare Workers' near-zero cold start a consequence of its execution model rather than just "good engineering" — what specifically is different from a container/VM-based FaaS platform?
11. A pipeline moves 10TB out of AWS and 10TB back from GCP every month. Roughly estimate the annual egress cost using the cheatsheet's rate ranges, and name two cost line items beyond the headline per-GB rate that a naive estimate would miss.
12. **Interview question:** Design a multi-cloud strategy for a company that wants to avoid vendor lock-in. What are the hidden costs?

---

## Answers

1. A **zonal** cluster runs its control plane (API server, etcd, scheduler) in a single zone — if that zone has an outage, existing pods keep running but you lose the ability to `kubectl apply`, scale, or schedule anything until the zone (or the control plane) recovers. A **regional** cluster runs the control plane replicated across three zones with automatic failover, backing GKE's 99.95% SLA. This matters operationally because "my pods are still running" during a zonal control-plane outage can mask that you've lost the ability to respond to an incident that *also* requires a deploy/scale/config change during that same window.
2. (a) Control-plane and node-group upgrades on EKS are two separate manual operations (`eksctl upgrade cluster` then `eksctl upgrade nodegroup`), with a hard constraint that node groups can't drift more than one minor version behind the control plane — nothing auto-upgrades the way GKE's release channels do. (b) You cannot inspect or exec into the GKE control plane on either cloud, but EKS additionally requires you to explicitly manage the OIDC provider association yourself for IRSA to even function, versus GKE's Workload Identity being enabled with a single cluster-creation flag.
3. Autopilot is a **cluster-wide operating mode** — every node on the cluster is Google-managed, billing switches to per-pod-resource-request, and cluster-wide policy restrictions (no privileged pods, no hostPath, etc.) apply everywhere. Fargate profiles are **scoped to specific namespaces/pod-label selectors** on an otherwise normal EKS cluster that still has regular EC2-backed managed node groups running everything else — you can mix Fargate-backed and node-backed workloads on the same EKS cluster, which isn't possible with Autopilot's all-or-nothing model.
4. Most likely cause: the VPC CNI's ENI-based pod IP allocation has hit its per-instance-type ceiling — `t3.medium` and similar smaller instance types support a limited number of ENI-backed IPs regardless of remaining CPU/memory. Fix: enable **prefix delegation** (`ENABLE_PREFIX_DELEGATION=true` on the VPC CNI), which assigns /28 IP prefixes instead of individual IPs per ENI slot, multiplying effective pod density per node without changing instance type.
5. Both solve "a pod needs to call a cloud API without embedding a long-lived static credential" — the pod gets short-lived, automatically rotated credentials tied to a Kubernetes ServiceAccount identity. The concrete difference: GKE Workload Identity federates trust via a **GCP IAM policy binding** (the Kubernetes SA is granted the `workloadIdentityUser` role on a GCP service account); EKS IRSA federates trust via an **OIDC provider association plus an IAM role trust policy** (the cluster's OIDC issuer is registered with AWS IAM, and a role's trust policy allows a specific K8s SA to assume it via STS).
6. A fleet/Arc-enabled resource is the **management-plane grouping and governance layer** applied across potentially many clusters on different infrastructure — it's what lets you push one GitOps config, one policy set, or one monitoring pipeline to every registered cluster regardless of where it runs. It's not just a renamed cluster because registering a cluster into a fleet or connecting it via Arc doesn't change how the cluster itself runs workloads — it adds an outbound-only agent connection and makes the cluster a target for centrally-managed policy/GitOps/observability tooling that didn't exist before registration.
7. The management cluster runs Cluster API's controllers and reconciles the desired state of *other* clusters (creating, scaling, upgrading, deleting them) — it is not where your application workloads run. `clusterctl move` (pivoting) exists because the initial management cluster is often a throwaway local `kind` cluster used only to bootstrap the first "real" clusters; pivoting migrates the CAPI custom resources (and therefore management responsibility) onto one of the workload clusters itself, so the temporary bootstrap cluster can be discarded once a durable, self-hosting management cluster exists.
8. The most common cause is that each cluster's `istiod` was installed with its **own independently self-signed root CA** instead of every cluster's intermediate CA being signed by one shared root. Remote secrets and healthy east-west gateways only handle *service discovery and routing* between clusters — they don't establish a shared trust domain. The fix is generating one root CA and per-cluster intermediate certs from it *before* installing Istio anywhere (or delegating this to cert-manager + `istio-csr` for centrally managed issuance), so every cluster's workload certificates chain back to the same trusted root.
9. During a real local outage, traffic stays pinned to the (now unhealthy) local endpoints and requests fail outright, because `localityLbSetting.failover` only defines *where* to fail over to — it has no way to detect that failover is needed without `outlierDetection` actively monitoring endpoint health (e.g., consecutive 5xx errors) and ejecting unhealthy endpoints from the load-balancing pool. Locality preference and failure detection are two separate, both-required pieces of the mechanism.
10. Workers execute inside **V8 isolates** — the same lightweight sandboxing mechanism Chrome uses for browser tabs — which share a running V8 process and start in single-digit milliseconds because there's no OS boot, container runtime initialization, or VM hypervisor involved. A container/VM-based FaaS platform (Lambda, Cloud Run) has to provision or resume an OS-level execution environment on a cold invocation, which is the actual source of cold-start latency; isolates sidestep that layer entirely.
11. Using the cheatsheet's blended tiers (~$0.07–0.09/GB outbound from AWS, ~$0.07–0.12/GB outbound from GCP on the return leg): roughly $700–900/month AWS-side plus roughly $700–1,200/month GCP-side ≈ **$1,400–2,100/month**, or **roughly $17,000–25,000/year** for this single pipeline. Two commonly missed line items: NAT Gateway per-GB-processed fees (billed on top of the headline egress rate for any private-subnet traffic routed through a NAT Gateway) and load-balancer data-processing charges (metered separately from both compute and the egress rate) — a naive estimate using only the advertised per-GB internet-egress number will materially undercount the real bill.
12. **Worked answer** — structure this like any senior system-design answer: clarify, then architecture, then the specific hidden costs, stated with real mechanisms and numbers, not just the word "expensive."
    - **Clarify first**: is "avoid lock-in" about a specific service category (compute/Kubernetes, managed database, ML platform) or the whole stack? Is this active multi-cloud (workloads split across clouds now) or passive (portable architecture, but running on one cloud today with the *option* to move)? I'll answer for the common real case: **passive portability as the default, active multi-cloud only where a specific requirement justifies its cost** — because full active multi-cloud for its own sake is usually the wrong default, and a strong answer says so explicitly rather than presenting multi-cloud as an unconditional good.
    - **Architecture for portability**: standardize on Kubernetes as the compute abstraction (CNCF-conformant, runs identically on GKE/EKS/AKS/on-prem) rather than cloud-specific serverless/PaaS primitives where avoidable; use Cluster API so cluster lifecycle tooling is provider-agnostic from day one, not bolted on later; use Terraform/OpenTofu with provider-agnostic module boundaries instead of deeply cloud-specific IaC patterns; prefer open-source/portable data stores (Postgres, Kafka) over proprietary managed equivalents where the managed convenience isn't worth the lock-in for that specific data.
    - **Where actual multi-cloud (not just portability) is justified**: genuine regulatory/data-residency requirements per region that different clouds serve better; negotiating leverage in vendor contract renewals; specific best-of-breed capabilities (e.g., a particular ML/data product only available on one cloud) worth the operational cost of running it alongside a primary cloud. Name this as a deliberate, narrow decision — not "run everything everywhere."
    - **The hidden costs, concretely** (this is the part of the answer that separates a strong candidate from one reciting "vendor lock-in is bad"):
      - **Egress economics**: cross-cloud data transfer is billed as full-rate internet egress on both sides, with no inter-cloud discount tier — a moderate data pipeline moving 10TB/month between two clouds can run $15-25k/year in egress alone (walk through the napkin math from file 4/Q11 if asked to go deeper), and this scales with data volume, which grows over time.
      - **NAT Gateway and load-balancer data-processing fees**: metered on top of the headline egress rate, on every cloud, and consistently the most underestimated line item in a naive multi-cloud cost model.
      - **Duplicated operational surface**: running the "same" workload on two clouds doubles the number of IAM models, networking models, and observability integrations a platform team has to understand deeply — this is a real, ongoing engineering-hours cost, not a one-time setup cost, and it's easy to underweight versus the more visible dollar-denominated infra costs.
      - **The abstraction tax**: building to the lowest-common-denominator API across clouds (to stay portable) means giving up cloud-specific optimizations and managed-service conveniences that would otherwise reduce operational burden and cost on a single cloud — portability itself has a cost, and a good answer names this explicitly as the fundamental tradeoff, not a side effect.
      - **Cross-cloud observability and DR replication costs are standing, recurring costs**, not one-time migration costs — shipping logs/metrics to a centralized stack in a different cloud, or continuously replicating data for DR, bills egress every month indefinitely.
    - **Close with the tradeoff, stated unprompted**: the honest recommendation is architecting for *portability* as the default (Kubernetes, Terraform, open-source data stores, avoiding deep proprietary coupling where a portable alternative is genuinely comparable) while treating *active* multi-cloud as a narrow, deliberately justified exception — not a blanket strategy — because the hidden costs above (egress, NAT/LB fees, duplicated ops surface, the abstraction tax) are real and recurring, and "avoid lock-in" is not free just because it sounds strategically prudent.
