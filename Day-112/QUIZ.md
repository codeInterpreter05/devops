# Day 112 — Quiz: Platform Engineering Concepts

Try to answer without looking at your notes. Answers are at the bottom.

1. What specific problem ("cognitive load") does platform engineering claim to solve, and why does it get worse as an organization scales past a handful of teams?
2. Name the five things a real IDP usually bundles together. Why isn't a catalog/portal alone considered "a platform"?
3. What does "Platform as Product" mean in practice? Give two concrete behaviors a platform team following this mindset would exhibit.
4. In Team Topologies, how does a platform team differ from an enabling team?
5. What is a golden path, and why is the "paved road" metaphor specifically about ease and speed rather than a hard technical block?
6. What are the two common implementation patterns for self-service infrastructure? Give one advantage of each.
7. Why is "guardrails baked into what the self-service interface exposes" considered better than "guardrails enforced by manual review after a request is submitted"?
8. What three Backstage pillars does today cover, and what specific problem does each one solve?
9. In a Backstage Scaffolder template, what do the `fetch`, `publish`, and `register` steps each do, and why does skipping `register` recreate toil the Scaffolder was meant to remove?
10. Why is TechDocs' docs-as-code approach (Markdown in the same repo, rendered in-portal) considered better than a separate wiki?
11. **Interview question:** What is an Internal Developer Platform? How does it reduce cognitive load on developers?

---

## Answers

1. Platform engineering targets the total cognitive load required for a developer to ship safely to production — under "you build it, you run it" DevOps, every app team independently needs competence in Kubernetes, IAM, Terraform, CI/CD authoring, and observability, on top of building features. This gets worse with scale because duplicated effort, inconsistent security postures, and overloaded developers compound across dozens of teams instead of just one.
2. A catalog/portal, self-service provisioning, golden-path templates/scaffolding, standardized CI/CD pipelines, and built-in observability/security defaults. A catalog/portal alone is just a directory/UI layer — without self-service provisioning and golden paths behind it, there's nothing actually reducing the underlying infrastructure complexity a developer has to deal with.
3. It means treating internal developers as customers with real choice: the platform team does product discovery (talks to dev teams about actual pain points instead of guessing), and measures success with product metrics (golden-path adoption rate, time-to-first-deploy, developer satisfaction) rather than just infrastructure metrics. A platform team practicing this would run something like regular user interviews with app teams, and would treat low adoption of a golden path as a signal to improve the product, not a discipline failure to punish.
4. A platform team provides an ongoing, ideally self-service, API/interface that reduces cognitive load for stream-aligned teams on a continuing basis. An enabling team engages temporarily to help a stream-aligned team adopt a new capability or skill, then disengages — it's a short-term coaching relationship, not an ongoing service.
5. A golden path is the easiest, most supported, well-documented way to accomplish a common task. The paved-road metaphor is about ease/speed rather than a hard block because forcing adoption through rigid technical restrictions breeds resentment and workarounds — the goal is for developers to choose the golden path voluntarily because it's genuinely better, with off-road (unsupported) options still technically possible.
6. Kubernetes-native self-service (CRDs reconciled by a controller, e.g. Crossplane) — advantage: fits naturally into a GitOps/Kubernetes-centric platform and gets RBAC/admission-control guardrails for free. Portal-driven self-service (a UI form triggering a pipeline) — advantage: doesn't require developers to know or write any YAML/CRDs at all, lowering the barrier further for less infra-savvy teams.
7. Because guardrails baked into the interface itself mean the unsafe option simply doesn't exist as a choice (structural safety) — there's nothing to forget or skip. Guardrails enforced by manual review depend on a human catching every violation every time, which doesn't scale and is exactly the kind of ticket-driven bottleneck self-service is meant to eliminate.
8. Software Catalog (solves "what services exist and who owns them" — a queryable inventory instead of tribal knowledge), TechDocs (solves "docs live somewhere nobody looks/maintains" via docs-as-code rendered in-portal), and Scaffolder (solves "creating a new service safely and consistently" by turning a golden path into a fillable form that produces a fully wired repo).
9. `fetch` templates a skeleton repository with the parameters the developer supplied; `publish` actually creates the real repo (e.g., on GitHub) with that content; `register` adds the new repo's `catalog-info.yaml` to the Backstage catalog automatically. Skipping `register` means every new service needs a manual catalog PR afterward — recreating the exact "who owns this and is it tracked" toil the whole point of the Scaffolder was to remove.
10. Because docs live in the same repo and the same PR review/version-control flow as the code they describe, so a code change and its doc update ship and get reviewed together. A separate wiki has no such coupling, so it silently rots because updating it isn't part of anyone's normal workflow — TechDocs removes that failure mode structurally rather than relying on discipline.
11. Strong answer: "An Internal Developer Platform is the layer of self-service tooling, APIs, and golden-path templates a dedicated platform team builds so application teams can provision infrastructure, deploy, and observe their services without needing deep expertise in the underlying systems like Kubernetes or cloud IAM. It reduces cognitive load by absorbing that complexity into a small number of well-designed, reusable abstractions — a developer requests a database via a simple self-service form or CRD instead of learning Terraform and cloud networking, and gets a pre-wired CI pipeline, observability setup, and catalog registration for free via a golden-path template instead of hand-rolling all of it themselves. The key is that this only works if the platform team treats it as a product — doing user discovery, measuring adoption, and making the golden path genuinely better rather than mandating it — otherwise you just relocate the bottleneck instead of removing it."
